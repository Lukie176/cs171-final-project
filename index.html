Ready. Set. Hike.

A look into the risk factors of injuries in the NFL.

Just as any other NFL Football season, avid football fans across the nation have continued to cheer their favorite teams into the endzone. Despite the incredible plays, viewers have no doubt noticed several high profile players, such as Dak Prescott, Christian McCaffrey, and Nick Chubb, become sidelined due to injury. Many would be quick to blame these injuries on this year’s unorthodox conditions, so let's take a look at how this year's injuries, from Week 1 to Week 9, compare to past years.

Notice that this year's overall number of injuries is consistent with past years.

Feel free to also explore this visualization. Hover over the bars to see the number of times that injury appeared on injury reports of that year. Filter by injury using the dropdown below.

NFL viewers might have been quick to assume that injuries in this season would be noticeably higher due to a lack of preseason training and preparation. However, as seen above, this season's total injuries are consistent with previous years. While the prevalence of injuries hasn't risen, football is still one of the most injury prone sports. As players are carted off the field and fans are left heartbroken (whether out of concern for the player or knowing that he won’t be making you fantasy points anytime soon), it’s important that we broaden our understanding as fans of this sport, especially when it comes to the well-being of players we have come to know, love, and cheer for.

One of the most pertinent factors to understand is that risk of injury is centralized to certain body parts. At first glance, we can see that NFL players injure almost every part of the body. However, lower-body injuries, particularly to the knee, ankle, and thigh, are especially prevalent in the NFL. In this visualization, you can see a breakdown of how many times each injury appeared on past injury reports. In addition to particular body parts, while some positions tend to be more publicised than others, it's also important to understand that the risk of injury isn't equitable across positions. We invite you to interact with the visualization below and learn about positions that interest you.

Feel free to hover over a position to see that position's name and the most frequently injured player this year that plays that position. Clicking on a position will give you a breakdown of that position's injuries by week over this season and the most common injuries they have suffered.

As you interact with this visualization, notice that defensive positions seem to take more punishment and injury than offensive positions, with Defensive Backs having the highest rate of injury.

Whether you’re born to a dynasty of Vikings fans or are just breaking into your love for the Steelers, a player's team is also a determinant of injury risk. Differences can be partially explained by different playbooks that teams live by, but by looking at injuries by team, we can at least see where the most risk lies.

Feel free to click on teams to see how they fare. Notice that the New York Jets have been consistently among the most injury prone teams in the NFL over the past few years. The Los Angeles Rams, on the other hand, tend to have very few injuries on their weekly injury reports.

Throughout these visualizations, we've revealed that total injuries this year are consistent with past years, lower-body injuries are the most prevalent type of injury, defensive positions have the most risk of injury, and the New York Jets are consistently a highly injured team.

With a broader understanding of the risk that these players take on, as well as the many determinants of injury, we encourage you to be mindful of what is at stake when tackles are made and routes are run. Football is an exhilarating sport to watch, but the enjoyment isn’t without a cost.

As such, we invite you to explore the resources put forth by the NFL Players Association and the National Athletic Trainers Association, two organizations leading the charge in developing NFL safety precautions.
